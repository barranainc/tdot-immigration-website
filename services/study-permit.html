What is a Study Permit?

A study permit is the document that authorizes most foreign nationals to study in Canada for a program longer than six months. Clients often still call it a study visa, but in practice the file may involve both a temporary resident visa or eTA for travel and a study permit for lawful study after arrival. TDOT helps applicants understand that distinction early so the file is framed correctly from the start.

A strong study permit application does more than prove admission. It must show that the school is a designated learning institution, that the program makes sense for the applicant’s academic or professional history, that the applicant has access to sufficient funds, and that the temporary stay remains credible under Canadian immigration law. Weak explanations around purpose of study, gaps in education, or unclear financial arrangements can trigger concerns even when the applicant has been accepted by a school.

Who Needs One

Most international students need a study permit when the program in Canada is longer than six months. Shorter programs may not require one, but many students still choose a permit route when there is a realistic chance of continuing studies or transitioning later. TDOT reviews the applicant’s full plan so that the chosen route supports long-term compliance.

Applicants should also understand when a temporary resident visa, eTA, or biometric appointment will be part of the file. A student who is changing status from within Canada or extending at the same DLI and level may face a different document strategy than a first-time applicant outside Canada.

Eligibility Requirements

IRCC generally expects a valid letter of acceptance, proof of available funds, identity documents, and evidence that the applicant will comply with study permit conditions. Depending on nationality and personal history, biometrics, police certificates, or medical exams may also become relevant. TDOT does not treat these items as a simple list; the team checks how the documents work together to support a consistent narrative.

Applicants with academic gaps, prior refusals, complex funding sources, or family ties in Canada often need additional explanation. A well-prepared cover letter and supporting package can help the officer understand why the plan is genuine, financially viable, and consistent with the applicant’s background.

Provincial Attestation Letter (PAL) — 2024/2025 changes

IRCC introduced a cap on study permit processing in 2024 and, for 2025, announced a target of 437,000 study permits. Most capped applicants needed a provincial or territorial attestation letter confirming access to one of the available spaces. IRCC also extended PAL/TAL requirements in 2025 to master’s and doctoral students and to most applicants applying from within Canada, while continuing exemptions for certain groups such as primary and secondary students and some in-Canada extensions at the same DLI and level.

IRCC guidance later clarified that, starting January 1, 2026, applicants to degree-granting master’s or doctoral programs at public DLIs generally do not need a PAL/TAL. Because these rules can shift, TDOT checks the current IRCC guidance and the school’s own process before the file is submitted. That matters because a PAL usually must be filed with the application, not after.

Documents Required

Most files will include a valid passport, letter of acceptance, tuition information, financial evidence, identity documents, and a detailed explanation of the applicant’s study plan. Depending on the case, TDOT may recommend employer letters, family support declarations, tax records, bank histories, educational transcripts, and a stronger purpose-of-study narrative.

The best document package is not the largest package. It is the package that answers the officer’s likely questions before they become concerns. TDOT organizes the file in a way that improves readability and shows how each piece of evidence supports the application.

How TDOT Helps

TDOT reviews program fit, checks whether a PAL or TAL is required, prepares the supporting letter, and helps the applicant present finances and personal history coherently. This is particularly valuable for mature students, career changers, or applicants who need to explain a gap between their previous studies and their proposed Canadian program.

The team also helps clients avoid preventable missteps, including contradictory timelines, generic purpose letters, and incomplete financial documentation. Good strategy before filing is often what separates a clean approval from a refusal that later requires damage control.

Study-to-PR Pathways

Many students are not only thinking about admission; they are thinking about what comes after graduation. TDOT helps clients understand how a compliant study plan can connect to a post-graduation work permit, Canadian work experience, the Canadian Experience Class, Express Entry, or a provincial nominee pathway. This kind of planning should be realistic rather than sales-driven.

Canada’s policies can change, especially around PGWP eligibility and provincial intake priorities. TDOT therefore frames study-to-PR planning as a strategic map, not a promise, and helps clients build flexible options at each stage.

FAQ

Questions clients often ask

Is a study permit the same as a study visa?

Not exactly. Clients often say study visa, but the permit authorizes study in Canada, while a TRV or eTA may govern travel to Canada. TDOT checks which combination applies to your case.

Do I always need a PAL?

No. Many applicants did need a PAL or TAL during the cap years, but IRCC lists exemptions and has adjusted the rules over time. TDOT checks the current requirement before filing.

Can prior refusals affect my study permit?

Yes. A previous refusal can influence how the officer reads your credibility, purpose of study, or financial evidence. It should be addressed carefully rather than ignored.

Can my spouse or child come with me?

Often yes, but eligibility depends on current policy, your program, your school, and your family’s travel plans. TDOT can map the family strategy alongside the main application.

Can a study permit lead to permanent residence?

A study permit can support later options such as PGWP, Canadian work experience, Express Entry, or PNPs, but it is not a guarantee of permanent residence. Strategy matters at every stage.
