Types of work permits

Canada broadly issues employer-specific work permits and open work permits. Employer-specific permits tie the worker to a named employer, occupation, and in many cases a location. Open work permits, where available, allow broader flexibility and may be available to spouses, certain graduates, or applicants in specific public-policy or transitional categories.

The right category is not always obvious. Some applicants assume an open work permit exists when it does not, while others miss an exemption that could simplify the process. TDOT reviews the facts carefully before the application is built.

LMIA process overview

Many employer-specific work permits depend on a positive Labour Market Impact Assessment. In simple terms, the employer must show that hiring the foreign national will not negatively affect the Canadian labour market and that recruitment and wage requirements have been met. A weak LMIA foundation can sink the permit application before the worker’s documents are even assessed.

TDOT helps clients understand how the LMIA record, job offer, and applicant qualifications must align. When the employer side is inconsistent, it creates avoidable risk for the applicant.

International Mobility Program exemptions

Not every work permit needs an LMIA. The International Mobility Program includes LMIA-exempt streams that serve broader policy goals, such as reciprocal employment, significant benefit, or intra-company transfer categories. These exemptions still require precise legal framing and supporting evidence.

Applicants often underestimate how carefully the exemption has to be documented. TDOT helps explain why the exemption applies and what documents are needed to support it.

PGWP for graduates

Graduates of eligible Canadian programs may qualify for a post-graduation work permit, but eligibility can depend on the institution, the program, and current IRCC policy. This is one reason TDOT encourages study permit clients to think ahead before choosing a school or course.

Because a PGWP can create the bridge to Canadian work experience and Express Entry, mistakes at the study stage can echo later. Planning the full sequence matters.

Spousal Open Work Permit

Spousal open work permit eligibility has changed over time and should never be assumed. The principal applicant’s status, the level of studies or work, and the current policy framework all matter. TDOT helps families present a strategy that addresses timing and eligibility together.

This is especially important where spouses want to travel separately, apply from different countries, or transition from visitor status inside Canada.

Bridging Open Work Permit

A bridging open work permit can help certain applicants maintain the ability to work while a permanent residence file is in process. The category is useful but technical: timing, application stage, and class eligibility must line up properly.

TDOT checks whether a bridging option exists and whether the underlying PR file is at the right stage before recommending it.

How TDOT Helps

TDOT reviews the permit class, supporting employer or family evidence, status timelines, and long-term PR implications before the application is filed. This helps clients avoid choosing a permit route that looks plausible online but does not match the law or the evidence.

For applicants with previous refusals, overstays, or status complications, the team also evaluates whether there are restoration or admissibility concerns that should be addressed at the same time.

FAQ

Questions clients often ask

What is the difference between an open and a closed work permit?

An open work permit usually allows broader employment flexibility, while an employer-specific permit ties you to a named employer and conditions. Availability depends on the legal category.

Do all employers need an LMIA?

No. Some work permits are LMIA-exempt under the International Mobility Program, but the exemption must be genuine and properly documented.

Can I apply from inside Canada?

In some cases yes, but eligibility depends on your current status and the category you are using. TDOT reviews this before filing.

Can a work permit lead to permanent residence?

Often it can support later PR options by creating Canadian work experience or employer support, but it is not an automatic pathway. Strategy matters.

What if I had a previous refusal?

A previous refusal does not always end the process, but the next application should address the earlier concerns directly and with better evidence.

Need the right work permit category before you apply?

TDOT can assess whether your route is LMIA-based, LMIA-exempt, open, spousal, graduate, or connected to a PR transition.
