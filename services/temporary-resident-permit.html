Temporary Resident Permit — Entry Despite Inadmissibility

A Temporary Resident Permit can allow travel to Canada despite inadmissibility when there is a compelling reason to enter and the circumstances justify the exception. TRP cases require careful legal framing, especially where the urgency is personal, professional, or humanitarian.

What a TRP is

A Temporary Resident Permit is a discretionary document that can allow a person who is otherwise inadmissible to enter or remain in Canada for a specific purpose. It is not the same as rehabilitation, a pardon, or a standard visitor visa. It is a remedy used when the need for entry is compelling enough to justify temporary admission despite the inadmissibility issue.

That means the application must address both sides of the equation: the reason for entry and the nature of the inadmissibility.

When it applies

TRPs are often considered where a person is inadmissible but needs to attend a family emergency, important business matter, legal proceeding, or other significant event in Canada. The strength of the reason matters, but so do the facts surrounding the inadmissibility. Not every urgent trip will justify the permit.

TDOT helps clients evaluate whether a TRP is the right strategy or whether rehabilitation, an ARC, or another remedy should be considered instead.

How to apply

A persuasive TRP file explains the inadmissibility clearly, provides supporting records, and demonstrates why the purpose of travel is compelling. Officers will often look for evidence that the applicant understands the issue, has been candid, and is presenting a focused request rather than a vague wish to travel.

TDOT helps structure the application so the legal issue and the practical reason for travel are both easy to understand.

Duration and limitations

A TRP is temporary and discretionary. The duration can vary depending on the circumstances and the decision-maker’s assessment. Because it is not a permanent solution, applicants should also consider whether a longer-term remedy such as rehabilitation may be available.

TDOT helps clients understand both the immediate objective and the longer-term immigration consequences.

How TDOT helps

TDOT assesses the inadmissibility ground, the urgency and legitimacy of the travel purpose, and the best way to document the request. In many cases, the success of a TRP file depends on the quality of the explanation and the supporting documentation rather than on volume alone.

That is why careful legal framing matters. A rushed or overly generic TRP request can do more harm than good.

Why clients call TDOT

TRP applications are discretionary. TDOT helps clients explain not only the inadmissibility issue, but also why entry is justified in spite of it and how the risks are being managed.

FAQ

Questions clients often ask

Is a TRP permanent?

No. A Temporary Resident Permit is temporary and discretionary. It does not erase the underlying inadmissibility.

Can I get a TRP because I want to visit family?

Sometimes, but the reason must be strong enough in context and the application must address the inadmissibility issue directly.

What is the difference between a TRP and rehabilitation?

A TRP allows temporary entry despite inadmissibility, while rehabilitation aims to resolve certain criminal inadmissibility issues more permanently.

Can TDOT help decide whether I need a TRP or another remedy?

Yes. That initial assessment is one of the most important parts of the strategy.

What if I was refused before?

A previous refusal changes the context and should be addressed directly with a stronger, more focused application.

Need urgent entry despite inadmissibility concerns?

TDOT can assess whether a Temporary Resident Permit is the right remedy and help present the strongest possible request.
